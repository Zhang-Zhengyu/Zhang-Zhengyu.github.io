Part A

1.       Definition with personal insight

1.1.      Literature review

Team building has evolved significantly since its inception. Originally designed as a group process intervention to improve interpersonal relationships and social interaction (Schein, 1969). It has since shifted its focus to include achieving results, meeting goals, and accomplishing tasks (Payne, 2001). By the end of the 1990s, team building had become a popular and common interaction (Salas et al., 1999), also known as team development or group development.

According to Klein et al. (Klein et al., 2009), team building is defined as a combination of formal and informal team-level practices that aim to improve social relations and clarify roles while also addressing tasks and interpersonal problems that affect team functioning. Based on Fapohunda (Fapohunda, 2013), team building is a generic term for a wide range of activities intended to improve interpersonal relationships and clarify roles within teams, frequently involving joint tasks. These activities are distinct from team training, which focuses on improving efficiency rather than interpersonal relations.

These definitions all emphasize that the purpose of team building is to improve interpersonal relationships, clarify roles, and address issues affecting team functioning through various activities. The differences between them lie in their different emphases on the goals and methods of team building.

1.2.      My definition of team building

The goal of team building is to strengthen interpersonal relationships and define responsibilities while addressing the tasks and interpersonal issues that have an impact on team performance. It consists of both formal and informal team-level practices. It is a broad phrase that refers to a variety of actions or procedures that strengthen interpersonal ties and outline team duties.

The main goal of team building is to bring out the best in a group of people by encouraging personal growth, effective communication, leadership qualities, and the capacity to collaborate closely to solve challenges.

 

2.       Theories and models

2.1.1.     Four approaches/components to team building (Potnuru et al., 2018)

(1)  Goal setting. It refers to establishing objectives for both team and each individual. The foundation of any team is usually a shared objective. The goal(s) may be vague and unclear when teams are first being formed. However, team objectives must be clear, doable, and quantifiable, as well as consistent with the general course. In accordance with the aforementioned standards, each person should also define their own goals. The purpose of this section is to increase team members' drive to accomplish the aims and objectives of the group (Stanton et al., 2004). Team members are expected to participate in action planning to determine how to reach those objectives (Aga et al., 2016).

(2)  Role-clarification. This part involves outlining and determining the particular responsibilities, expectations, and conventions of each team member. Tasks clarification can be used to improve team and individual characteristics and work organization by negotiating, defining, and changing team member duties (Mathieu & Schulze, 2006). Because it enables managers to match what needs to be done to the person most qualified to accomplish the work at a high level, clear individual task allocation is important. The notion that one's labor benefits others, or task significance, has repeatedly appeared as one of the strongest indicators of meaningful employment.

(3)  Interpersonal relations. For a team to work well, communication and trust-building are vital. Teams that experience less interpersonal conflict outperform teams that experience more conflict. It takes enhanced teamwork abilities, including communication, sharing of emotions, and mutual support. Conflict frequently occurs in teamwork, especially during the team's early storming phase (Tuckman, 1965). Although I would first see every disagreement among team members as unpleasant and detrimental, the process of resolving disagreements can actually increase the team's effectiveness. The way a team views a conflict and how it impacts the team's progress toward its objectives may decide whether or not it is productive.

(4)  Problem solving. The fourth component places a strong emphasis on identifying and fixing significant issues in team activities in order to enhance task-related competencies. In this intervention, team members identify significant issues, produce pertinent information, engage in problem-solving, plan actions, carry them out, and assess their efficacy.

2.1.2.     Nine team roles

In order for a team to succeed, Dr. Meredith Belbin (Belbin, 1996) identified nine team roles: Plant, Resource Investigator, Co-ordinator, Shaper, Monitor Evaluator, Teamworker, Implementer, Completer Finisher, and Specialist. Each role has specific advantages and disadvantages that can be used to determine which one is best for each team member.

3.       Applicability of team building to engineering students

In the engineering field, both educators and employers place a great priority on teamwork abilities. The formation of student teams for course projects is common, especially in laboratory, design, and research programs, as project teams are an integral component of our workplace culture. For instance, there are various divisions of labor in a robot design project, including mechanical structure design, control system design, industrial design etc. Although these divisions are independent of one another, they must work together to form a complete robot.

 

Part B

4.       Self-experience

In my first year at PolyU, I had an unfortunate group project experience. Our goal was to create a mathematical model to predict diabetic patients, requiring a report and group presentation. I hastily agreed to join friends for the project, not considering potential free-riders or uncooperative members. This was my first mistake. Then, we divided the work, but I miscalculated the difficulty of data-preprocessing, overlooking problematic data in the database. My role involved merging and editing the report, relying on my peers to send their sections. However, some members submitted poor-quality work, not meeting requirements, and even generated by AI. I requested revisions and clarified expectations, but some members refused to modify their reports, leading to conflicts and even quarrels. This hindered group unity and progress.

5.       View sharing and self-reflection

From my perspective, the above story is a failed teamwork experience, even if the assessment was uploaded on time with the combined efforts of one member and me. The final time before deadline is very tight and rushed. That made me pretty anxious and panicky. Right after I uploaded all the assessment, I felt relaxed all over.

In this group project, all four approaches to team building are represented (Potnuru et al., 2018). Goal setting is fulfilled at the time of team formation. Role-clarification is the part that didn��t do well. I underestimated the difficulty of data-preprocessing and the impact that problematic data could have on the project. However, it's avoidable through working together to go through the whole process of the project before assigning tasks. This ensures that everyone has a common understanding of the details of the project, and also allows me to assign tasks more accurately and appropriately.

Interpersonal relationships are also well represented in this experience. Dealing with poor work from team members can be a challenge, but it is important to communicate my expectations and the requirements of the project clearly and respectfully with my teammates. More importantly, it is a prerequisite for effective communication to first acknowledge the efforts of teammates. My experience has taught me that I need to show enough respect and encouragement to those who are not willing to cooperate. It is also important to resolve any conflicts that arise in a constructive manner to avoid negatively impacting the progress of the group.

Problem solving in team building should contain two parts: problems in project, problems in team management (Fapohunda, 2013). In my experience with this team, both of these problems existed simultaneously. I encountered technical difficulties, including difficulty processing problem data and lack of high model accuracy. Team management was also a big problem: low quality work, failure to complete it at the appointed time, and difficulty in getting feedback.

6.       Self-understanding

According to Belbin��s nine role model (Belbin, 1996), my role in the above experience could be co-ordinator, monitor evaluator and finisher. My different roles in the team demonstrate my strengths and weaknesses. I have demonstrated my strengths in my professional abilities, and I have fully demonstrated my ability to communicate and integrate in a team as a coordinator. However, the work on this project also demonstrates my weaknesses in time management and effective communication.

7.       The way to improve my team building skills and personal development:

I can start by carefully selecting teammates for upcoming group projects. It's critical to consider each team member's abilities, work ethic, and willingness to participate in the project. I must also communicate my expectations to the team members and set clear guidelines for the project. To do this, specify roles and responsibilities, set deadlines, and communicate project requirements. I ought to promote candid communication and handle any disagreements respectfully and constructively. It's critical to promote a supportive environment for teamwork, encourage collaboration, and aid in each other's advancement. I can be proactive in problem-solving by foreseeing potential obstacles and creating plans to overcome them before they become significant problems.

Engineering programs require building a team almost all the time. By improving the quality of team building, engineering students like me can have better communication skills, enhance problem-solving ability, and improve time management.

Conclusion

In conclusion, team building is an essential component of contemporary society that entails a mix of formal and informal team-level activities. The four elements of team building��goal setting, role clarification, interpersonal relations, and problem-solving��as well as the nine crucial team roles identified by Dr. Meredith Belbin have all been covered in this review, along with other theories and models. Engineering is one of many fields where teamwork skills are useful because they are highly valued by educators and employers. From personal experience, it is evident that in order to produce the desired outcomes, team building calls for a variety of qualities, including effective communication, trust, and problem-solving abilities.
